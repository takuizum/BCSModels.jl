MCMC vs. Mean-field CAVI for BCSM — empirical comparison

Empirical results comparing the closed-form Gibbs sampler and the new mean-field CAVI inference engines from this package. Both engines are run on the same simulated data, with the same priors. Generated by scripts/sim_mcmc_vs_vb.jl and aggregated by scripts/analyze_results.jl. The raw CSV is at results/sim_reps20.csv.

Experimental design

  • 20 simulated replicates per cell.
  • MCMC: Gibbs with niter = 1000 after burnin = 500 (closed-form conditionals; see derivations.md).
  • VB: mean-field CAVI with maxiter = 400, tol = 1e-7. 95% credible intervals from the Gaussian approximation m_θ ± 1.96·√v_θ.
  • IRT-BCSM cells (single covariance layer): N ∈ {200, 500, 1000}, K ∈ {10, 20}, true θ ∈ {0.2, 0.4, 0.6}.
  • Testlet-BCSM cells (multi-layer): T ∈ {3, 4} testlets, true θ_t in [0.15, 0.5].

Conclusion

The user's framing is partly confirmed and partly refuted by the data.

What the experiment confirms:

  • MCMC clearly outperforms VB on every accuracy metric (bias, RMSE, CI coverage).
  • VB carries a systematic negative bias on every covariance component: it under-estimates θ consistently, with the bias growing in θ_true and in the number of additive layers.
  • VB severely under-estimates the posterior SD — the median variational SD is 41 % of the Gibbs posterior SD, and the variational 95 % credible interval covers the truth only 18 % of the time (vs. the nominal 94 % for MCMC).

What the experiment refutes or qualifies:

  • "VB is close to MCMC" only holds for the single-layer IRT-BCSM at small θ (e.g. RMSE 0.03 vs 0.03 at N=500, θ=0.2). For testlet-BCSM with multiple layers and θ near 0.5, VB is off by 40–45 % of the truth — that is not "close" in any operational sense.
  • "VB is practically useful" is true if "practical" means point-estimate screening at 10× speed, but false if it means valid uncertainty quantification — the credible intervals are not usable for inference.

So the working summary should read something like:

MCMC is the reference; VB matches it well only for simple single-layer BCSM with weak dependence. For practically relevant testlet settings VB shows a systematic 30 – 45 % negative bias on the covariance components and recovers only ~18 % nominal-95 % coverage. VB is 10–20× faster and useful as a screening tool, but not as a stand-alone inference engine.

Why this happens — and what would fix it

The mean-field factorisation q(z) = ∏_{i,j} q(z_{ij}) removes the within-testlet correlation between latent utilities. That correlation is exactly what carries the signal for θ_t, so its expectation E_q[Q_t] in the CAVI update for q(θ_t) is systematically too small, biasing E_q[θ_t] downward and shrinking Var_q[θ_t] proportionally.

Two concrete upgrades likely close most of the gap:

  1. Block-structured variational family q(z_i) = N_K(μ_i, S_i) with S_i parameterised by the same additive BCSM structure as the model. This preserves the within-testlet correlation under q and removes the dominant bias source. The CAVI for q(θ_t) then has the same sufficient statistic the Gibbs uses, so we expect bias to drop to the MCMC level.

  2. Importance-weighted ELBO (Cho et al., 2024, Psychometrika) to correct the residual bias from any remaining mean-field assumptions.

Both are natural research follow-ups; neither is implemented in this release.
